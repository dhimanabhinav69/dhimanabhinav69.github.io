slide 03 / business problem

The analytical problem

Developer survey data is rich but messy. Many fields are multi-select, respondents come from different experience levels and geographies, and the same tool can appear in multiple contexts. The challenge is to create a dashboard that remains simple while preserving the depth of the survey.

  • Which developer roles dominate the respondent base?
  • Which programming languages and tools are used together?
  • How do experience level and compensation bands relate?
  • Where are developers learning: formal education, online courses, documentation, or community forums?
  • How visible is AI adoption across developer workflows?

A survey workflow built for exploration and storytelling.

02

Split multi-select fields for languages, databases, platforms, frameworks, tools, and AI usage into tidy analysis tables.

03

Compare

Compared technologies across experience bands, developer roles, and compensation ranges to identify adoption patterns.

04

Visualise

Built survey views for role mix, technology adoption, learning channels, compensation distribution, and AI usage signals.

05

Explain

Converted charts into a concise narrative for recruiters, product teams, and early-career developers planning skill investments.

slide 05 / insights

What the dashboard is built to reveal.

01

Skill clusters

Technology selections can be grouped into skill clusters, showing which languages, databases, cloud tools, and frameworks tend to appear together.

02

Career-stage differences

Experience bands help separate beginner-friendly learning signals from senior-level tool preferences, making the dashboard useful for career planning.

03

AI adoption context

AI usage becomes more meaningful when compared with role type, years of experience, and workflow category rather than treated as one isolated metric.

slide 06 / recommendations

Business actions suggested by the analysis.

For recruiters

Use role-specific technology clusters to write sharper job descriptions and avoid mixing unrelated skill requirements in one posting.

For learners

Prioritise tool combinations that appear together in real developer workflows instead of studying technologies as isolated keywords.

For product teams

Segment adoption by role and experience before designing developer-focused documentation, onboarding, or community programs.

For analysts

Keep survey dashboards filter-first, because the same technology trend can tell different stories across geography, role, and experience.
